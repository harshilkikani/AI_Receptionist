## Two-presenter choreography

**Body language.** A stands or sits **slightly forward**, looking at the audience. B is **half a step back**, behind or beside the laptop, screen angled toward the audience — not toward B. If you're at a table, B sits to A's right, laptop centered between them and angled toward the prospect.

**The handoff rule.** A talks while B types. B never narrates while typing — wait for the AI response, then make space for A. If A has stopped talking and the AI is still composing, **let the silence happen.** Pointing at the screen with an open hand draws the eye for free.

**The recovery rule.** If anything weird happens — slow response, odd phrasing, a wrong-fact moment — A acknowledges it lightly and reframes. B does not apologize, doesn't hover over the screen, doesn't start frantically clicking. See the recovery playbook below.

**The silence rule.** Don't fill every gap. The strongest demo moments are when the AI is replying and nobody is talking — the audience reads the screen, and the screen does the work.

## The optional live phone call moment

This is the single most persuasive 60 seconds in any demo. Use it whenever the room is engaged and time allows.

**Setup**

- Have the notecard with **+1 (844) 940-3274** ready in your pocket.
- Confirm the laptop demo is paused or quietly running — you don't want chat dings during a live call.
- Hand the card to the prospect, not to one of your team. They dial. They control the conversation.

**The pitch**

**A:** "Honestly, I'd rather have you hear her than have me tell you. This number's a working production line. Put it on speaker, ask anything — pricing, hours, an emergency scenario, whatever. Sixty seconds."

**During the call**

- **Don't coach.** Let the prospect drive. Whatever they ask, the AI handles or it doesn't — that's the truth of the product.
- **Don't apologize.** If the AI says something imperfect, that's real and they'll respect it more than a polished demo.
- **Don't talk over the AI.** Even if the prospect asks you a side question. Point at the phone, mouth "let her finish."

**After the call**

**A:** "That's the same engine you'd have on your line. We change the company name, the services, your cell. About 15 minutes of setup."

**If something went off**

If the AI flubbed during the live call — wrong fact, weird filler, slow response — name it lightly:

**A:** "She's tuned for one of our HVAC tenants right now, so the answer to your real-estate question came out a little crooked. On your own line she's trained on your business. But you heard the tone — that's what matters."

Don't hide it. Owning the imperfection makes the rest of the demo more credible.

## Recovery playbook — what to say when things go sideways

**The AI is slow to respond.**

A: "Real model, real internet — about as fast as a person reading the message." Don't apologize. Pointing out that the latency is human-paced reframes it.

**The AI says something factually weird.**

A: "She's tuned for the demo tenant right now — every owner trains her differently. On your shop's line she'd know your services cold." Frame as a tuning thing, which is true.

**The AI uses an awkward filler.**

A: "Bit of personality there." Smile, move on. Don't dwell. The audience moves on if you do.

**The page glitches, refresh stalls, or owner SMS doesn't appear.**

B reloads quietly. A keeps talking: "Working software, working internet — we don't fake any of this so occasionally something hiccups. Let me tell you the part you'd see in production while she catches up."

**The demo won't load at all.**

Pivot to the live call. A: "Honestly, the demo's how we show all three pieces side by side, but the part that matters most you can just hear. Try the number." Hand them the notecard.

**Wi-Fi dies.**

Hotspot. B handles it without narrating. A keeps the room: "While we get the laptop back — the thing about this product that I find people don't expect is..." (Buy 30 seconds with a story about a customer.)

**A specific feature isn't behaving today.**

Skip it. A: "There's also a feature for [X] — I'll walk you through it after if you want, but the part that matters most is what you just saw." Cutting silently is better than fumbling visibly.

**The prospect catches the AI in an obvious mistake.**

A: "Yeah, fair catch. You'd see that in your transcript review and tune it the next morning — these things get better daily, not slower." Own it. The product is in fact reviewed and improved daily — say that, and mean it.

## Improv opportunities

These are the moments where the demo stops being a demo and starts being a conversation.

**The tenant switcher.** "What kind of business are you in?" → B switches the industry live. The chat suggestions update. The demo personalizes in one click.

**A custom message.** "What's a typical question you get?" → B types it into the chat. The AI handles their actual question with their industry's tone.

**Their name.** "What's your name? Watch — I'll have her use it." → B types a message that prompts the AI to use their name. They hear their own name in the AI's reply. It lands hard.

**Their nightmare call.** "What's the worst call you got last month?" → B types the scenario. Audience sees what their AI would've done with it. This is the single most persuasive improv move — the prospect is literally watching their pain be handled.

**Their pricing.** "Roughly what does a service call cost at your shop?" → B uses the tenant switcher and types something. AI quotes a number in their range. They see how it'd sound on their line.

**Their objection.** "If a customer asked you [their objection], how would you want this handled?" → They tell you. You type a version of it. They watch the AI handle it the way they would've.

## Pacing — where to slow down, where to let it breathe

| Moment | Pace | Notes |

| First AI reply | Don't talk | Let the audience read the screen. |

## What to avoid

- **AI buzzwords.** Don't say "leverage," "powered by AI," "machine learning," "natural language processing." Owners glaze over. Say "she" or "the receptionist."
- **Model names.** Never say GPT, Claude, Anthropic, OpenAI by name. It pulls them out of the demo and into vendor evaluation mode.
- **Architecture.** Don't talk about Twilio, FastAPI, SQLite, ElevenLabs. They don't care. The product is the receptionist, not the stack.
- **Feature dumping.** Pick three moments per demo — the emergency, the memory, the portal — and let those land. Adding a fourth feature dilutes the three that work.
- **Apologizing.** If something goes wrong, name it, reframe it, move on. Don't apologize, don't get small.
- **Pitching.** No "imagine if..." no "wouldn't it be great if..." Show the thing. Let it speak.
- **Talking over the AI.** The single most common mistake. The AI's voice is the product. Your voice introduces it.

**Same day:**

Send them their own demo tenant link via SMS or email. One sentence:

## Variants by audience

**Real estate associations / agent meetups.**
Open with real estate, not HVAC. Skip the emergency flow. Spend more time on the after-hours capture and the memory moment (buyers come back). Mention the unified call+text timeline twice — agents track this manually and hate it.

**HVAC / plumbing / trades trade shows.**
Open with HVAC emergency. The owner SMS is the money shot. Spend more time on the operator portal — these are operators, they think in dispatch and routing.

**Small business networking, mixed industries.**
Use the tenant switcher live. "What do you do?" → switch industry → demo. Treats every prospect like the demo was built for them, because it was.

**Investor 1:1s.**
Same flow but compress the demo to 8 minutes and add 4 minutes on traction, unit economics, and what the next 6 months look like. The demo's job in an investor meeting is to prove the product is real — once they believe that, get back to the business.

**Coffee meetings with a single owner.**
Slow down. Use the full 12 minutes. Ask about their actual business between beats. End on the one-week pilot.

---

## Final principle

The best demos feel **operational, not theatrical.** When the prospect walks away, the thing they should remember isn't a slick presentation — it's the moment the owner's phone buzzed three seconds after the customer typed, and they thought: *that would actually work in my business.*

Nothing in this script matters more than that one moment landing. Pace the entire demo toward it. Everything else is scaffolding.
